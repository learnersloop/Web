Faith, Storms, and the Irony of Humanity: My Reflections on A Letter to God

Published on May 31, 2026 | By Aditya Tiwari

We have all heard about faith. Our elders talk about it, books preach it, and sometimes we feel it. But it was not until I sat down and thoroughly read "A Letter to God" by G.L. Fuentes—the very first chapter of our English textbook—that I truly began to look at faith, humanity, and irony in a completely new light.

As a student diving into this story, it did not just feel like another chapter to prepare for exams. It felt like a deep, slightly bittersweet lesson about how the world works. Here is my personal take, breakdown, and reflection on Lencho's world.

The Setting and the Hope of a Farmer

The story introduces us to Lencho, a hardworking farmer whose house sits alone on the crest of a low hill. From there, he can look at his fields of ripe corn, dotted with kidney bean flowers. To me, Lencho represents the ultimate symbol of resilience. He is an ox of a man, working tirelessly, completely dependent on nature.

All his field needed was a downpour, or at least a sharp shower. I could practically feel his anticipation when he looked toward the northeast, predicting rain. And it did rain! Big drops began to fall, and for Lencho, these were not just water droplets—they were new coins. The big drops were ten-cent pieces, and the little ones were fives. It is beautiful how Fuentes shows that for a poor farmer, rain is not just weather; it is prosperity, food, and life.

When Nature Turns Cruel: The Destruction

But the story takes a dark turn, and this is where my heart sank for Lencho. Suddenly, a strong wind began to blow, and along with the rain, truly large hailstones began to fall. Fuentes uses a brilliant contrast here. While Lencho's children ran out to collect the frozen pearls with joy, Lencho's anxiety peaked.

The hail rained for an hour on the house, the garden, the hillside, and the entire cornfield. The field was white, as if covered with salt. Not a leaf remained on the trees. The corn was totally destroyed. The flowers were gone from the plants. Lencho's soul was filled with sadness. He stood in the middle of his ruined field and told his sons that even a plague of locusts would have left more than this. That night was a sorrowful one. All their hard work had gone to waste, and they faced the bleak reality of starvation.

Unshakable Faith: Writing to the Ultimate Authority

This is the turning point of the chapter that absolutely fascinated me. In Lencho's mind, there was only one hope left: help from God. His faith was not passive; it was incredibly active and concrete. He did not just pray into the night. On the following Sunday, at daybreak, he decided to write a literal letter to God. Imagine that! Putting pen to paper, addressing it to the Almighty, and walking to town to post it.

In the letter, he wrote that if God did not help him, his family and he would go hungry this year. He requested a hundred pesos to sow his field again and to live until the next harvest. He wrote "To God" on the envelope, put the letter inside, and dropped it into the mailbox.

"True understanding comes when you connect your textbook chapters to real-world human behavior."

Enter the Postmaster: A Glimmer of True Humanity

When the postman found the letter, he laughed heartily and showed it to the postmaster. At first, the postmaster—a fat, amiable fellow—broke out laughing too. But almost immediately, he turned serious. He tapped the letter on his desk and remarked on the sheer brilliance of Lencho's faith. He wished he had the same faith of the man who wrote the letter, starting up a correspondence with God!

Instead of shaking Lencho's faith by throwing the letter away or ignoring it, the postmaster decided to act. He came up with an idea: answer the letter. But opening it revealed that answering required more than just good will, ink, and paper. It demanded money. Sticking to his noble resolution, he gave up part of his own salary, asked his employees for contributions, and even got several friends to give something for an act of charity. He could not manage to gather the full hundred pesos, but he managed to get a little more than half—seventy pesos. He put the money in an envelope addressed to Lencho and signed it with a single word as a signature: GOD.

The Peak of Irony and My Final Thoughts

What happens next is what makes "A Letter to God" a timeless masterpiece. The following Sunday, Lencho arrived a bit earlier than usual. The postman himself handed him the letter. Lencho showed not the slightest surprise upon seeing the money—such was his absolute confidence! But when he counted the money, he became furious. God could not have made a mistake, nor could He have denied Lencho what he had requested!

Lencho immediately went up to the window, asked for paper and ink, and wrote another letter to God with a heavily wrinkled brow. He posted it, and the moment it fell into the mailbox, the postmaster opened it. The letter read that of the money he asked for, only seventy pesos reached him. He requested the rest since he needed it very much, but asked God not to send it through the mail, because the post office employees are a bunch of crooks.
